Hip Hop or House?

Posted on July 17, 2025
Read Full Analysis

This project explores the sonic and cultural distinctions between Hip Hop and House music—two genres with shared DNA but unique identities. Both heavily rely on digital sampling of existing music, with live instrumentation being a rarity outside of performances. It’s common to find hip hop producers sampling house beats, and electronic artists using hip hop vocals or basslines. These elements often serve as creative foundations or accents within a broader composition.

Despite occasional overlap, each genre contains a wide array of subgenres that blur stylistic boundaries. To draw distinctions, I considered not only production methods but also the cultural values that shaped each sound.

Hip Hop, as a cultural movement, encompasses MCing (rapping), DJing, breakdancing, and graffiti. A key distinction is the prominence of the MC—lyrics are central. The voice carries meaning, emotion, and narrative. Conversely, House music, rooted in club culture and popularized in venues like Paradise Garage by figures like Larry Levan, prioritized rhythm, inclusivity, and the dance floor over lyrical storytelling.

In production terms, House often favors stripped-back arrangements with emphasis on groove and atmosphere. Vocals are optional and frequently processed into textures. Hip hop, on the other hand, prioritizes vocal clarity, which limits how much processing can occur without compromising intelligibility. This difference is evident in spectral characteristics.

Recording Methodology

All audio clips were digitized from vinyl using a mixer and interface, then captured in Ableton Live at 44.1kHz, 16-bit resolution. As analog recordings, surface noise—clicks, pops, and groove inconsistencies—was expected. These artifacts are visible in the waveform data but don’t meaningfully affect analysis.

Clips were selected to represent each genre from my personal perspective. To challenge the classifier, I included edge cases—instrumental hip hop tracks to reduce vocal bias, and vocal-heavy house tracks to test beyond rhythmic features. Certain Kanye West songs were included for their compressed, almost house-like qualities.

Analysis Parameters

Spectral Flatness
Flatness measures how noise-like a signal is. High flatness suggests white-noise characteristics—a flat response across frequency bands. Low flatness indicates tonal content, such as sine waves. House music often shows higher flatness due to its percussive, noise-rich textures.

Entropy
Spectral entropy quantifies the “peakiness” of a signal and helps identify vocal formants. It’s commonly used in voice activity detection. Here, it highlights vocal vs. instrumental sections by identifying structured formants versus erratic noise responses.

Brightness
Brightness refers to the concentration of high-frequency energy. House music, with roots in disco, often features bright synths and glistening textures. Hip hop, by contrast, tends toward lower frequencies, gritty vocals, and bass-heavy mixes.

Pulse Clarity
This measures rhythmic predictability. House music’s four-on-the-floor beat typically yields high pulse clarity. Hip hop, more rhythmically varied due to layered vocals and syncopated beats, often results in lower clarity.

Root Mean Square (RMS)
RMS measures average loudness over time. While House is perceived as louder, this may relate more to playback context (clubs, festivals) than production. Measuring RMS helps explore whether that perception holds true technically.

Results

Flatness vs. Entropy

Figure A shows that Hip Hop and House tracks occupy similar regions in flatness and entropy. Notable outliers include:

  • ‘Babasonic.wav’ (1,7) – drastically lower flatness and entropy, likely due to being part of a breakdown.
  • ‘C.R.E.A.M (2).wav’ (2,33)– low-vocal outro, closest neighbour to low scoring house track ‘Babasonic’.
  • ‘Money Trees.wav’ (Hip Hop) – high entropy and flatness due to continuous vocals and noise-like instrumentation.

These comparisons show both genres can fluctuate between noise-like and tonal depending on the song segment.

Spectral Flatness (Detailed Visuals)

The flatness visuals for two clips—‘Money Trees’ (Hip Hop) and ‘Please Forgive Me’ (House)—offer a clearer comparison than scatter plots, showing distinct peaks and troughs. ‘Money Trees’ displays pronounced peakiness, while ‘Please Forgive Me’ features a flatter response.

Entropy Detail

Figure C helps distinguish vocal formants presence. ‘C.R.E.A.M’ (Hip Hop) shows entropy peak before 4 seconds, separating vocal from instrumental content. ‘Funky Introductions’ (House) shows a steady pattern without sharp peaks, indicating absence of vocal formants.

Pulse Clarity vs RMS

Figure D illustrates pulse clarity and average loudness (RMS). Two Hip Hop outliers: Kanye West’s ‘Gorgeous’ (2.37) and Kendrick Lamar’s ‘Duckworth’ (2.34). ‘Gorgeous’ reflects modern compression with loud, polished sound. ‘Duckworth’ has lower RMS, aligning with its intimate narrative style.

Brightness and Pulse Clarity

Figure E shows Hip Hop tracks cluster centrally, House tracks higher and right—brighter and rhythmically clearer. This fits House’s minimal structure and Hip Hop’s layered vocal complexity.

Mean Parameter Values

Figure F shows average parameter values almost identical across genres except pulse clarity, which stands out. Hip Hop is layered with dense vocals/instrumentation; House features minimal arrangements.

Areas of Training and Further Findings

Figure G tests classifier on female vocals absent in training. Correctly identifies ‘into_you.wav’ as Hip Hop, with entropy and RMS above average, brightness, pulse clarity, and flatness near or below average.

Figure H shows a heavily compressed track, with elevated entropy and RMS, lower pulse clarity, brightness, and flatness, matching gritty, processed sound. Classified as Hip Hop correctly.

Figure I, instrumental clip tests classifier robustness without vocals. Similar to Hip Hop average RMS and pulse clarity but lower brightness.

Figure K tests classifier with borderline house/techno track, showing flatter response and higher loudness but lower brightness.

Project Summary

This project examined acoustic and structural distinctions between Hip Hop and House through analysis of audio features: RMS, entropy, pulse clarity, brightness, and flatness. Visualizations revealed shared traits and key differences.

Key Differences:

  • Acoustic Texture: Hip Hop has higher peakiness and entropy, reflecting layered vocals and dynamic instrumentation.
  • Tonality and Structure: House features flatter spectral responses, lower entropy, and higher pulse clarity consistent with minimal arrangements.
  • Loudness and Dynamics: Hip Hop’s vocals give wider dynamic range; House maintains steadier loudness.

Similarities:

  • Both share overlapping frequency ranges and rhythmic roots.
  • Instrumentation overlaps in sampled basslines and drums but with different usage.

Cultural and Creative Context:

Hip Hop emphasizes storytelling and vocal presence; House leans into danceable grooves and atmosphere. The genres influence each other while retaining identities. This analysis shows how acoustic parameters mirror cultural distinctions but also highlights challenges in computational genre classification.
